# Croissants — A Primer on Lamination

Croissants are a laminated dough, meaning a block of butter (the *beurrage*) is folded into a yeasted dough (the *détrempe*) through successive turns to create hundreds of alternating thin layers. On baking, water in the butter converts to steam and pushes the layers apart, producing an airy, honeycombed interior and a flaky crust.

A classic process uses three "letter folds" (single turns) with rest periods in between. The dough is kept cold throughout: if the butter softens too much it merges with the dough and the layers collapse.

Common pitfalls:
- Butter too cold: it shatters during the fold.
- Dough too warm: butter leaks out during proof.
- Under-proofed: dense, bready interior.
- Over-proofed: collapsed structure and greasy bake.

A well-laminated croissant has a distinct honeycomb crumb and a crisp, shatter-when-pressed crust.
